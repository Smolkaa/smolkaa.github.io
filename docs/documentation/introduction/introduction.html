1. Introduction

Author
Affiliation

Alexander Smolka

Technical University of Munich

Published

November 9, 2023

Space exploration has always captivated humanity’s imagination, driving scientific inquiry and technological advancement to unprecedented heights. While the Moon’s surface has been a focal point of human exploration, its tenuous exosphere, a fragile envelope of particles and gases, has long remained an understudied frontier. Recent decades have witnessed remarkable strides in our understanding of extraterrestrial exospheres, with a particular focus on the Moon’s exosphere and its intricate interactions with the lunar surface.

Advances in space exploration have unveiled the Moon’s exosphere as a realm of astonishing complexity. Notable missions such as the Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter (LRO) have played a pivotal role in unraveling the mysteries of the lunar exosphere. The orbiter mission has not only provided the most highly resolved images of the Moon’s surface, see Fig. 1 showing high-resolution mosaic images of the lunar’s north and south poles, but the Lyman Alpha Mapping Project (LAMP) (Gladstone et al., 2009) aboard LRO has studied the exosphere by analyzing the scattering of ultraviolet sunlight. Additionally, the Lunar Atmosphere and Dust Environment Explorer (LADEE) mission’s Neutral Mass Spectrometer (NMS) (Benna et al., 2015) provided valuable insights into the composition of the exosphere and its temporal variations. These missions collectively painted a new picture of the Moon - a dynamic world with a delicate exosphere shaped by intricate surface interactions.

However, the first endeavors to investigate the lunar exosphere were already made decades earlier, during the Apollo program, during which the Lunar Atmosphere Composition Experiment (LACE) instrument was deployed on the Moon’s surface by Apollo 17 as part of the Apollo Lunar Surface Experiment Package (ALSEP) (Chang, 1972). LACE marked the first direct detection of exospheric species, capturing trace amounts of noble gases and demonstrating the existence of a lunar atmosphere. This historic endeavor laid the foundation for our present understanding, highlighting the complexity of the lunar exosphere and the need for continued exploration.

In the broader scope of planetary science, beyond the lunar realm, lies a captivating tapestry of exospheric phenomena across our solar system. Among these, Mercury, the closest planet to the Sun, has beckoned explorers and researchers alike. Pioneering missions such as NASA’s MESSENGER (MErcury Surface, Space ENvironment, GEochemistry, and Ranging) have revealed the intricate details of Mercury’s exosphere. MESSENGER’s observations have unraveled the complex interplay between the planet’s surface and its tenuous envelope of gases, shedding light on the origins of exospheric constituents and their dynamic behavior in response to solar wind interactions. Through these missions, the study of exospheres extends its reach beyond Earth’s celestial companion, contributing valuable insights to the broader understanding of planetary atmospheres and their interactions with the space environment.

Background and Motivation

State of the Art

One of the first modelers of the lunar exosphere was Hodges et al. (1973), who analyzed light noble gases as well as volatile molecular hydrogen and compared their results with data from the Apollo missions. Their Monte-Carlo approach of tracking individual particles through the tenuous atmosphere until eventually lost to one of the implemented loss mechanisms was pioneering work that still inspires new models today. With Chamberlain (1963), Armand (1977), and Huebner et al. (1992) setting the theoretical foundation of exospheric density projections, thermal velocity distributions, and lunar exospheric photoreaction rates, respectively, models became increasingly adapted to predicting number densities in surface-bounded exospheres.

While previously, only single-species simulations with mostly noble gases were performed, Crider & Vondrak (2002) were among the first to investigate the highly reactive hydrogen exosphere, which includes reaction pathways to allow conversions from atomic hydrogen to more complex molecules like water. Their research led to a heightened interest in the role of the planetary surface in exosphere densities and compositions. Farrell et al. (2015), Farrell et al. (2017), B. M. Jones et al. (2018), B. Jones et al. (2021), Tucker et al. (2018), Tucker et al. (2021), Grumpe et al. (2019) are among the leading researchers to investigate the proton/hydrogen interaction with lunar regolith, trying to untangle the highly complex physical and chemical reaction environment of the lunar surface.

A more complete list of relevant publications about the state-of-the-art modeling approaches regarding surface-bounded exosphere, their respective surface, various involved processes, and auxiliary research can be found in Table 1.

Reference Description / Highlights

Chamberlain (1963)

THEORY EXOSPHERE

“Planetary coronae and atmospheric evaporation”

  • analytical model of density distributions in planetary atmospheres/exospheres
  • assumes an isothermal, spherically symmetric, and globally uniform atmosphere
  • developed a scaled barometric scaling for exospheric number densities at varying altitudes

Vogel (1966)

THEORY EXOSPHERE

“Molecular fluxes in the lunar atmosphere”

  • theoretical model of molecular fluxes in the lunar exosphere
  • proposed an analytical framework to calculate elliptical trajectories and the respective landing positions on the Moon

Hodges et al. (1973)

EXOSPHERE

“Composition and dynamics of lunar atmosphere”

  • first \(\ce{H2}, \ce{He}, \ce{Ne}\), and \(\ce{Ar}\) model of the lunar exosphere
  • compared model results with preliminary Apollo 17 data

Armand (1977)

THEORY

“Classical theory of desorption rate velocity distribution of desorbed atoms; Possibility of a compensation effect”

  • proposed the theory of desorption velocity distributions | (Maxwell-Boltzmann Flux Distribution, MBFD)

Smith et al. (1978)

EXOSPHERE

“Monte Carlo modeling of exospheric bodies: Mercury”

  • Monte-Carlo model of Mercury’s exosphere
  • MBFD distributed particle velocities

Huebner et al. (1992)

THEORY

“Solar photo rates for planetary atmospheres and atmospheric pollutants”

  • provides a comprehensive overview of photoreaction rates widely used for modeling the lunar exosphere

Butler (1997)

THEORY EXOSPHERE

“The migration of volatiles on the surfaces of Mercury and the Moon”

  • Monte-Carlo exosphere model of Mercury and the Moon, specifically \(\ce{H2O}\) and \(\ce{CO2}\)
  • proposed an analytic function to approximate the lunar surface temperature
  • extension of Vogel (1966) trajectory calculation

Crider & Vondrak (2002)

EXOSPHERE

“Hydrogen migration to the lunar poles by solar wind bombardment of the Moon”

  • Monte-Carlo simulation of exospheric hydrogen migration on the Moon
  • lists a variety of conversion paths for solar wind protons to \(\ce{H}\), \(\ce{H2}\), \(\ce{OH}\), and \(\ce{H2O}\)
  • comments on the high inaccuracies of the implementation of the chemical and physical reactions

Chaufray et al. (2009)

EXOSPHERE

“Lunar Argon cycle modeling”

  • Monte-Carlo model of the lunar argon exosphere
  • investigated the accumulation of \(\ce{Ar}\) in PSRs

Poppe et al. (2013)

EXOSPHERE

“The self-sputtered contribution to the lunar exosphere”

  • first self-sputtering model of the lunar exosphere
  • self-sputtering is a significant source of neutrals (locally comparable to solar wind and micrometeoroid bombardment)

Grava et al. (2014)

EXOSPHERE

“Lunar exospheric argon modeling”

  • Monte-Carlo model of the lunar argon exosphere, based on Chaufray et al. (2009)
  • compared model predictions with LACE, LAMP, and LADEE measurements
  • found that charge exchange is a relevant argon loss process and that cold-trapping losses are comparable to ionization fluxes

Schorghofer (2014)

EXOSPHERE

“Migration calculations for water in the exosphere of the Moon: dusk-dawn asymmetry, heterogeneous trapping, and D/H fractionation”

  • Monte-Carlo model of exospheric water migration of the Moon, based on Butler (1997)
  • presents a dusk-dawn asymmetry of hydrogenous species

Farrell et al. (2015)

SURFACE

“Solar wind implantation into lunar regolith: hydrogen retention in a surface with defects”

  • Monte-Carlo model of solar wind implantation into the lunar surface regolith
  • systematically studied distributions of activation energies to simulate hydrogen retention and \(\ce{OH}\) formation

Hurley et al. (2015)

THEORY

“An analytic function of lunar surface temperature for exospheric modeling”

  • presents an analytic approximation of the lunar surface temperatures based on Diviner measurements
  • improved the analytical approach of Butler (1997)
  • temperature model does not include topographical, temporal, or compositional features

Prem et al. (2015)

EXOSPHERE

“Transport of water in a transient impact-generated lunar atmosphere”

  • hybrid DSMC approach to investigate water transport in impact-generated lunar atmosphere, combining a continuum description of the dense plume with a Monte-Carlo model of the tenuous water vapor exosphere
  • unsteady and three-dimensional simulations

Hurley et al. (2016)

EXOSPHERE

“Understanding temporal and spatial variability of the lunar helium atmosphere using simultaneous observations from LRO, LADEE, and ARTEMIS”

  • Monte-Carlo model of the lunar surface-bounded helium exosphere
  • suggests that endogenic helium provides roughly a third of the Moon’s exospheric helium
  • built on previous works by Killen & Ip (1999), Hodges et al. (1973), Butler (1997), Grava et al. (2014), and more

Moores (2016)

EXOSPHERE

“Lunar water migration in the interval between large impacts: heterogeneous delivery to permanently shadowed regions, fractionation, and diffusive barriers”

  • Monte-Carlo simulation to examine water delivery to lunar PSRs
  • impactor water delivery (based on Prem et al. (2015)) and ballistic migration included as water sources
  • explicitly modeled the high-latitude regions, including several PSRs
  • extended Crider & Vondrak (2002) and Schorghofer (2014) for higher latitudes

Farrell et al. (2017)

SURFACE

“The statistical mechanics of solar wind hydroxylation at the Moon, within lunar magnetic anomalies, and at Phobos”

  • extension of the previous model (Farrell et al., 2015), presents a formalism to statistically describe hydrogen implantation into the lunar surface and subsurface diffusion of \(\ce{H}\) using a Monte-Carlo simulation
  • used distributed activation energies in the physical model

Hurley et al. (2017)

EXOSPHERE

“Contributions of solar wind and micrometeoroids to molecular hydrogen in the lunar exosphere”

  • Monte-Carlo model (extension of previous model (Hurley et al., 2016)) to investigate spatial distribution of \(\ce{H2}\) in the lunar exosphere
  • examined the contributions of micrometeoroids and solar wind particles
  • found that micrometeoroids are an insufficient source to reproduce LAMP \(\ce{H2}\) measurements

J. A. Kegerreis et al. (2017)

EXOSPHERE

“Evidence for a localized source of the argon in the lunar exosphere”

  • Monte-Carlo model of the lunar \(\ce{Ar}\) exosphere, based on the works of Smith et al. (1978), Hodges et al. (1973), and Butler (1997)
  • includes seasonal effects to study long-term variations

B. M. Jones et al. (2018)

SURFACE

“Solar wind-induced water cycle on the Moon”

  • chemical kinetics model of lunar surficial \(\ce{OH}\) and \(\ce{H2O}\), investigating the long-term evolution
  • based on solar wind proton delivery as the main source of the simulated water cycle
  • focuses on recombinative desorption as the primary water formation pathway

Prem et al. (2018)

THEORY SURFACE

“The influence of surface roughness on volatile transport on the Moon”

  • numerical thermal model for a rough lunar surface
  • extension of Hurley et al. (2015) thermal model

Tucker et al. (2018)

EXOSPHERE SURFACE

“Solar wind implantation into the lunar regolith: Monte Carlo simulations of H retention in a surface with defects and the H2 exosphere”

  • Monte-Carlo-based simulation of solar wind implantation into the lunar lunar regolith to study \(\ce{H}\) retention and implications for the \(\ce{H2}\) exosphere
  • modeled hydrogen diffusion and recombination to \(\ce{H2}\)
  • includes Gaussian distributions of activation energies
  • based on models by Killen & Ip (1999), Farrell et al. (2015), Farrell et al. (2017), and Hurley et al. (2017)

Grumpe et al. (2019)

SURFACE

“Time-of-day-dependent behavior of surficial lunar hydroxyl/water: Observations and modeling”

  • reaction kinetics model as an extension of Farrell et al. (2015) and Farrell et al. (2017) Monte-Carlo simulation
  • investigates \(\ce{H}\), \(\ce{OH}\), and \(\ce{H2O}\) concentrations, based on both solar wind and micrometeoroid influx
  • includes a 1D lateral heat conduction model with surface roughness dependence

Killen et al. (2019)

EXOSPHERE

“The lunar neon exosphere seen in LACE data”

  • Monte-Carlo simulation of the lunar neon exosphere
  • comparison with Apollo 17’s LACE data
  • suggests that theoretical photodissociation losses are too low to reproduce \(\ce{Ne}\) measurements

Prem et al. (2019)

EXOSPHERE

“Coupled DSMC-Monte Carlo radiative transfer modeling of gas dynamics in a transient impact-generated lunar atmosphere”

  • extension of the Monte-Carlo model of Prem et al. (2015) to include the influence of radiative heat transfer and shielding of an impact-generated atmosphere
  • applies new model to simulate a \(\ce{H2O}\) exosphere
  • finds that gas-gas radiative transfer is playing an important role, significantly influencing water vapor transport to the night-side
  • includes a chapter on numerical validations of the presented model

Grava et al. (2020)

EXOSPHERE

“LRO/LAMP observations of the lunar helium exosphere: constraints on thermal accommodation and outgassing rate”

  • Monte-Carlo simulation of lunar exospheric helium to investigate source rates and thermal accommodation
  • compares modelled \(\ce{He}\) densities with LAMP measurements
  • suggests best description of lunar helium assuming full thermal accommodation and a MBFD distribution of kinetic energies
  • finds that endogenic \(\ce{He}\) provides about 20% of the lunar helium exosphere as compared to solar wind source

J. Kegerreis (2020)

THEORY EXOSPHERE

“Planetary giant impacts, simulating collisions and their consequences”

  • Monte-Carlo simulation of the lunar argon exosphere and comparison with LADEE data
  • applied altitude fitting based on Chamberlain (1963) projection model to compare predicted densities with satellite data
  • uses the landing position calculation proposed by Butler (1997)

Schörghofer (2020)

THEORY EXOSPHERE

“Thermodynamics of surface-bounded exospheres”

  • thermodynamical investigation of exospheric densities based on various velocity distributions
  • provides statistical averaged and exact solutions for short ballistic flights, assuming constant gravitational acceleration

Grava et al. (2021)

REVIEW EXOSPHERE

“Volatiles and refractories in surface-bounded exospheres in the inner solar system”

  • review of exospheric models of surface-bounded exospheres in the inner solar system, focusing on the Moon and Mercury
  • considers various volatiles, refractories, ions, and ENAs

B. Jones et al. (2021)

SURFACE

“Thermal evolution of water and hydrogen from Apollo lunar regolith grains”

  • simulated TPD process and comparison with TPD experiment
  • considered several hydrogen-based species and their concentrations on the surface, in the subsurface and void space subsurface, and in gaseous form
  • specifically discussed recombinative desorption as the main pathway of water formation

Sarantos & Tsavachidis (2021)

SURFACE

“Lags in desorption of lunar volatiles”

  • computer-generated lunar regolith packing to model random walks of argon and water particles to simulate gas transport
  • suggests subsurface diffusion and desorption-readsorption of gas particles leads to a lag in the exospheric density signature

Schörghofer et al. (2021)

REVIEW SURFACE

EXOSPHERE

“Water group exospheres and surface interactions on the Moon, Mercury, and Ceres”

  • review of hydrogen-based exospheric particles and their interaction with their planetary surface
  • reports on the status of observations, theory, and laboratory measurements

Tucker et al. (2021)

SURFACE EXOSPHERE

“On the effect of magnetospheric shielding on the lunar hydrogen cycle”

  • Monte-Carlo model to investigate \(\ce{H}\) implantation, conversion to \(\ce{OH}\), and subsequent outgassing of \(\ce{H2}\)
  • simulates the Moon’s passage through Earth’s magnetotail, eliminating the solar wind proton source, to which the hydrogen both on the surface as well as in the exosphere respond quickly
  • general model based on previous work of Tucker et al. (2018)

Morrissey et al. (2022)

SURFACE

“Simulating the diffusion of hydrogen in amorphous silicates: a ‘jumping’ migration process and its implications for solar wind implanted lunar volatiles”

  • molecular dynamics simulation to describe the \(\ce{H}\) movement in amorphous silica on an atomistic level
  • uses insights to infer hydrogen diffusion in planetary regoliths
  • examines the degassing of water group particles and its influence on the lunar exosphere
  • baseline comparison with previous models of Farrell et al. (2015), Farrell et al. (2017), B. Jones et al. (2021), and Tucker et al. (2021)

Poppe et al. (2022)

EXOSPHERE

“A comprehensive model for pickup ion formation at the Moon”

  • two-part analytic model describing the lunar exospheric neutrals density distributions and the ionization and charged-particle dynamics of pickup ions based on the modeled neutral distributions
  • includes 18 different exospheric ion species

Schörghofer (2022)

THEORY EXOSPHERE

“Statistical thermodynamics of surface-bounded exospheres”

  • building on Schörghofer (2020)
  • provides an analytic description of velocity distributions and exact solutions for emerging density profiles

Wurz et al. (2022)

REVIEW THEORY

SURFACE

“Particles and Photons as Drivers for Particle Release from the Surfaces of the Moon and Mercury”

  • review of surface interactions of exospheric particles
  • includes space weathering, thermal releases, MMIV, PSD and ESD, physical and chemical sputtering, and particle reflections

Milillo (2023)

REVIEW EXOSPHERE

SURFACE

 “Future Directions for the Investigation of Surface-Bounded Exospheres in the Inner Solar System”
Schörghofer & Rufu (2023) “Future Directions for the Investigation of Surface-Bounded Exospheres in the Inner Solar System”
Schörghofer (2023) “Future Directions for the Investigation of Surface-Bounded Exospheres in the Inner Solar System”

Smolka et al. (2023)

EXOSPHERE

“Coupled H, H2, OH, and H2O lunar exosphere simulation framework and impacts of conversion reactions”

  • Monte-Carlo simulation framework to simultaneously model multiple hydrogen-based exospheric species
  • includes several, uncertain conversion reactions, coupling the species with each other
  • suggests that surface reaction with hydrogen can significantly change exospheric dynamics around the terminator regions

Teolis et al. (2023)

REVIEW EXOSPHERE

SURFACE

“Surface exospheric interactions”

  • review of descriptions, current developments, and issues of exosphere-surface interactions
  • includes discussions of thermal accommodation, volatile diffusion, and cold-trapping

The Problem of Scales

One of the most prominent problems for exosphere models is based on the enormous difference in physical scales between relevant processes. While the dimensions of the respective planetary object and its topographic features are around hundreds of kilometers, surface and subsurface processes like particle releases or diffusion are described on micro and sometimes down to atomic scales (Milillo, 2023).
