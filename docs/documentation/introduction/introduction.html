1. Introduction

Author

Alexander Smolka, a.smolka@tum.de
Technical University of Munich

Published

August 31, 2023

Space exploration has always captivated humanity’s imagination, driving scientific inquiry and technological advancement to unprecedented heights. While the Moon’s surface has been a focal point of human exploration, its tenuous exosphere, a fragile envelope of particles and gases, has long remained an understudied frontier. Recent decades have witnessed remarkable strides in our understanding of extraterrestrial exospheres, with a particular focus on the Moon’s exosphere and its intricate interactions with the lunar surface.

Advances in space exploration have unveiled the Moon’s exosphere as a realm of astonishing complexity. Notable missions such as the Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter (LRO) have played a pivotal role in unraveling the mysteries of the lunar exosphere. The orbiter mission has not only provided the most highly resolved images of the Moon’s surface, see Fig. 1 showing high-resolution mosaic images of the lunar’s north and south pole, but the Lyman Alpha Mapping Project (LAMP) (Gladstone et al., 2009) aboard LRO has studied the exosphere by analyzing the scattering of ultraviolet sunlight. Additionally, the Lunar Atmosphere and Dust Environment Explorer (LADEE) mission’s Neutral Mass Spectrometer (NMS) (Benna et al., 2015) provided valuable insights into the composition of the exosphere and its temporal variations. These missions collectively painted a new picture of the Moon - a dynamic world with a delicate exosphere shaped by intricate surface interactions.

See 3.1 Missions for more information on the missions mentioned here!

However, the first endeavors to investigate the lunar exosphere were already made decades earlier, during the Apollo program, during which the Lunar Atmosphere Composition Experiment (LACE) instrument was deployed on the Moon’s surface by Apollo 17 as part of the Apollo Lunar Surface Experiment Package (ALSEP) (Chang, 1972). LACE marked the first direct detection of exospheric species, capturing trace amounts of noble gases and demonstrating the existence of a lunar atmosphere. This historic endeavor laid the foundation for our present understanding, highlighting the complexity of the lunar exosphere and the need for continued exploration.

In the broader scope of planetary science, beyond the lunar realm, lies a captivating tapestry of exospheric phenomena across our solar system. Among these, Mercury, the closest planet to the Sun, has beckoned explorers and researchers alike. Pioneering missions such as NASA’s MESSENGER (MErcury Surface, Space ENvironment, GEochemistry, and Ranging) have revealed the intricate details of Mercury’s exosphere. MESSENGER’s observations have unraveled the complex interplay between the planet’s surface and its tenuous envelope of gases, shedding light on the origins of exospheric constituents and their dynamic behavior in response to solar wind interactions. Through these missions, the study of exospheres extends its reach beyond Earth’s celestial companion, contributing valuable insights to the broader understanding of planetary atmospheres and their interactions with the space environment.

State of the Art – Modeling

Table 1: Caption.
Reference Description / Highlights Connection
Chamberlain (1963)
  • analytical model of density distributions in planetary atmospheres/exospheres
  • assumes an isothermal, spherically symmetric, and globally uniform atmosphere
  • developed a scaled barometric scaling for exospheric number densities at varying altitudes
Vogel (1966)
  • theoretical model of molecular fluxes in the lunar exosphere
  • proposed an analytical framework to calculate elliptical trajectories and and the respective landing positions on the Moon
Hodges et al. (1973)
  • first \(\ce{H2}, \ce{He}, \ce{Ne}\), and \(\ce{Ar}\) model of the lunar exosphere
  • compared model results with preliminary Apollo 17 data
Armand (1977)
  • proposed the theory of desorption velocity distributions (Maxwell-Boltzmann Flux)
Smith et al. (1978)
  • Monte-Carlo model of Mercury’s exosphere
  • MBFD distributed particle velocities
Huebner et al. (1992)
  • provides a comprehensive overview of photoreaction rates widely used for modeling the lunar exosphere
Butler (1997)
  • Monte-Carlo exosphere model of Mercury and the Moon
  • investigated \(\ce{H2O}\) and \(\ce{CO2}\)
  • proposed an analytic function to approximate the lunar surface temperature
  • extension of Vogel (1966) trajectory calculation
Crider & Vondrak (2002)
  • Monte-Carlo simulation of exospheric hydrogen migration on the Moon
  • lists a variety of conversion paths for solar wind protons to \(\ce{H}\), \(\ce{H2}\), \(\ce{OH}\), and \(\ce{H2O}\)
  • comments on the high inaccuracies of the implementation of the chemical and physical reactions
Chaufray et al. (2009)
  • Monte-Carlo model of the lunar argon exosphere
  • investigated the accumulation of \(\ce{Ar}\) in PSRs
Poppe et al. (2013)
  • first “self-sputtering” model of the lunar exosphere
  • suggests that self-sputtering is a significant source of neutrals in the exosphere (locally comparable to solar wind and micrometeoroid bombardment)
Schorghofer (2014)
  • Monte-Carlo model of exospheric water migration of the Moon
  • presents a dusk-dawn asymmetry of hydrogenous species
  • based on Butler (1997)
Farrell et al. (2015)
  • Monte-Carlo model of solar wind implantation into the lunar surface regolith
  • systematically studied distributions of activation energies to simulate hydrogen retention and \(\ce{OH}\) formation
Grava et al. (2014)
  • Monte-Carlo model of the lunar argon exosphere
  • compared model predictions with LACE, LAMP, and LADEE measurements
  • found that charge exchange is a relevant argon loss process and that cold-trapping losses are comparable to ionization fluxes
  • based on Chaufray et al. (2009)
Hurley et al. (2015)
  • presents an analytic approximation of the lunar surface temperatures, based on Diviner measurements
  • temperature model does not include topographical, temporal, or compositional features
  • improved the analytical approach of Butler (1997)
Prem et al. (2015)
  • hybrid DSMC approach to investigate water transport in impact-generated lunar atmosphere, combining a continuum description of the dense plume with a Monte-Carlo model of the tenuous water vapor exosphere
  • unsteady and three-dimensional simulations
Hurley et al. (2016)
  • Monte-Carlo model of the lunar surface-bounded helium exosphere
  • suggests that endogenic helium provides roughly a third of the Moon’s exospheric helium
  • built on previous works by Killen & Ip (1999), Hodges et al. (1973), Butler (1997), Grava et al. (2014), and more
Moores (2016)
  • Monte-Carlo simulation to examine water delivery to lunar PSRs
  • impactor and ballistic migration included as water sources
  • explicitly modeled the high-latitude regions, including several PSRs
  • water delivery through impacts based on Prem et al. (2015)
  • extended Crider & Vondrak (2002) and Schorghofer (2014) for higher latitudes
Farrell et al. (2017)
  • formalism to statistically describe hydrogen implantation into the lunar surface and subsurface diffusion of \(\ce{H}\) using a Monte-Carlo simulation
  • used distributed activation energies in the physical model
  • extension of previous model (Farrell et al., 2015)
Hurley et al. (2017)
  • Monte-Carlo model to investigate spatial distribution of \(\ce{H2}\) in the lunar exosphere
  • examined the contributions of micrometeoroids and solar wind particles
  • found that micrometeoroids are an insufficient source to reproduce LAMP \(\ce{H2}\) measurements
  • extension of previous model (Hurley et al., 2016)
J. A. Kegerreis et al. (2017)
  • Monte-Carlo model of the lunar \(\ce{Ar}\) exosphere
  • compared densities with LADEE data
  • includes seasonal effects to study long-term variations
  • based on the works of Smith et al. (1978), Hodges et al. (1973), and Butler (1997)
B. M. Jones et al. (2018)
  • chemical kinetics model of lunar surficial \(\ce{OH}\) and \(\ce{H2O}\), investigating the long-term evolution
  • based on solar wind proton delivery as the main source of the simulated water cycle
  • focusses on recombinative desorption as the main water formation pathway
Prem et al. (2018)
  • numerical thermal model for a rough lunar surface
  • extension of Hurley et al. (2015) thermal model
Tucker et al. (2018)
  • Monte-Carlo based simulation of solar wind implantation into the lunar lunar regolith to study \(\ce{H}\) retention and implications for the \(\ce{H2}\) exosphere
  • modeled hydrogen diffusion and recombination to \(\ce{H2}\)
  • included Gaussian distributions of activation energies
  • included models by Killen & Ip (1999), Farrell et al. (2015), Farrell et al. (2017), and Hurley et al. (2017)

Killen et al. (2019)

  • comparison with Apollo 17’s LACE data
